Copyrighted Media Accessibility Remediation

Interactive decision flowchart for instructional designers at public universities remediating copyrighted videos, podcasts, and music for accessible online courses

1 Identify the Copyrighted Media

What type of media do you need to make accessible for your online course?

▼

Decision Is an accessible version already available?

Check the publisher, distributor, or platform (e.g., Kanopy, Swank, Films on Demand) for a version with captions, subtitles, or audio description.

▼

Action Use the accessible version

Obtain and use the already-captioned or described version. Verify caption quality meets WCAG 2.1 AA standards (accuracy, synchronization, speaker identification). Confirm your licensing agreement covers online course use.

Legal basis: ADA Title II (28 CFR 35.200); WCAG 2.1 AA Success Criteria 1.2.2, 1.2.3, 1.2.5

▼

Decision Does the TEACH Act apply?

View TEACH Act checklist
  • Institution is accredited and nonprofit
  • Use is under instructor supervision as part of mediated instructional activities
  • Content is directly related to and of material assistance to the teaching content
  • Only "reasonable and limited portions" are used (analogous to a live classroom display)
  • Access is limited to enrolled students via LMS authentication
  • Downstream copying is technologically prevented
  • Institution has a copyright policy and provides copyright notices
  • You are NOT using content primarily produced for the digital education market

17 U.S.C. § 110(2) — TEACH Act (2002)

▼

Action Caption under TEACH Act + Fair Use for Accessibility

You may caption the video as an accessibility remediation. The act of adding captions to copyrighted video for accessibility is generally supported by fair use (transformative purpose; no market substitution) when combined with TEACH Act coverage.

  • Create or commission accurate captions (99%+ accuracy target)
  • Include speaker identification and relevant non-speech audio
  • Add audio description track if visual content is essential
  • Store captioned version securely in your LMS; restrict access to enrolled students
  • Do not distribute the captioned file outside the course
  • Document your fair use analysis (keep on file)

17 U.S.C. § 107 (Fair Use); 17 U.S.C. § 110(2) (TEACH Act); ADA Title II

▼

Decision Does Fair Use support captioning?

Apply the four-factor fair use test:

View four-factor analysis for accessibility captioning
  • Factor 1 — Purpose: Nonprofit educational + accessibility accommodation = strongly favors fair use. The captioning is transformative (adds access, does not substitute for original purpose).
  • Factor 2 — Nature of work: Creative works (film, documentary) weigh slightly against fair use, but this is less significant when the purpose is accessibility rather than copying content.
  • Factor 3 — Amount used: You may need to caption the entire work. Courts have allowed using entire works when necessary for the purpose. For accessibility, captioning the full work is often unavoidable.
  • Factor 4 — Market effect: Captioning for enrolled students does not substitute for the market. You are not distributing a competing product. This strongly favors fair use.

Overall: Captioning copyrighted video for enrolled students with disabilities at a public university is widely considered fair use, especially when no accessible version is commercially available. The U.S. Copyright Office has acknowledged this principle.

17 U.S.C. § 107; U.S. Copyright Office guidance on accessibility

▼

Action Caption under Fair Use

  • Document your four-factor fair use analysis in writing
  • Confirm no accessible version is commercially available at a fair price
  • Create or commission WCAG 2.1 AA-compliant captions
  • Add audio description if visual content conveys essential information
  • Restrict access to enrolled students only
  • Store the fair use rationale with your course files

17 U.S.C. § 107; ADA Title II; Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act

▼

Caution Seek Permission or License

  • Contact the rights holder to request permission to caption
  • Check if your institution has a blanket license (e.g., CCC, Swank)
  • Negotiate captioning rights in your licensing agreement
  • If permission is denied, consult your university's Office of General Counsel (or equivalent) — ADA obligations may still require remediation
  • Consider replacing the content with an openly licensed alternative (Creative Commons, OER)

Note: ADA compliance obligations are not waived by copyright claims. Consult legal counsel if rights holder refuses permission.

▼

Decision Is a transcript already available?

Check the podcast publisher's website, RSS feed, or hosting platform for an existing transcript.

▼

Action Use the existing transcript

  • Verify transcript accuracy and completeness
  • Ensure speaker identification is included
  • Check that significant non-speech audio (music cues, sound effects) is described
  • Format transcript as accessible HTML or provide in an accessible document format alongside the audio
  • Provide synchronized captions if the audio is embedded in a media player

WCAG 2.1 AA Success Criteria 1.2.1 (audio-only prerecorded)

▼

Decision How are you using the podcast?

▼

Action Transcribe under Fair Use for Accessibility

Creating a transcript of a copyrighted podcast for student accessibility is a strong fair use case. A transcript is a different modality from the audio — it provides access, not a market substitute.

  • Create an accurate transcript with speaker labels
  • Note significant non-speech audio in brackets
  • Provide transcript alongside the link in your LMS
  • Restrict access to enrolled students
  • Document your fair use rationale

17 U.S.C. § 107; ADA Title II; WCAG 1.2.1

▼

Decision Does the TEACH Act cover the audio use?

For audio embedded or uploaded to the LMS, check the TEACH Act checklist (same as video). Only "reasonable and limited portions" of sound recordings are covered — not the entire recording for music/dramatic works.

▼

Action Transcribe under TEACH Act + Fair Use

  • Use the permitted portion under TEACH Act
  • Create accurate transcript for that portion
  • Restrict access via LMS to enrolled students
  • Apply DRM or copy-prevention as feasible
  • File your fair use analysis
▼

Action Transcribe under Fair Use for Accessibility

Even without TEACH Act coverage, fair use strongly supports creating a transcript for accessibility. Apply the four-factor test:

  • Purpose: nonprofit educational + accessibility = transformative
  • Amount: full transcription may be necessary for access
  • Market effect: a text transcript does not substitute for the audio experience
  • Create full transcript; restrict to enrolled students
  • Document fair use rationale
  • If the content is a dramatic work or music performance, consider licensing or replacing with OER

17 U.S.C. § 107; ADA Title II

▼

Important Music has additional copyright layers

Music involves multiple rights: the composition (publishing rights), the sound recording (master rights), performance rights (ASCAP/BMI/SESAC), and synchronization rights. Accessibility remediation for music in online courses requires careful analysis.

▼

Decision How is the music being used in your course?

▼

Action Describe in captions; no separate music remediation needed

  • In captions, describe the music contextually: [upbeat jazz music plays] or [somber orchestral music]
  • If the music conveys emotional or narrative meaning, include brief description in audio description track
  • You are not reproducing or distributing the music separately — no additional copyright clearance needed for the description
  • If lyrics are relevant to the content, include them in the captions

WCAG 1.2.2 (Captions); fair use covers descriptive captioning

▼

Decision What accessibility remediation is needed?

▼

Action Provide lyrics under Fair Use / TEACH Act

  • Use only the portion needed for the lesson (fair use factor 3)
  • Provide lyrics in an accessible format within the LMS
  • Cite the songwriter, publisher, and copyright year
  • For full lyrics in a music appreciation course, document your fair use analysis carefully (amount used may be the whole work, but educational purpose and restricted access support fair use)
  • Consider linking to licensed lyrics databases (e.g., services your library subscribes to)

17 U.S.C. § 107; 17 U.S.C. § 110(2)

▼

Action Create descriptive text analysis

  • Write original descriptive text analyzing musical elements (tempo, key, instrumentation, dynamics, form)
  • This is original scholarly commentary, not a reproduction — strong fair use
  • Include visual waveform or spectrogram representations if helpful
  • Pair with any available score excerpts (brief portions) under fair use
  • Collaborate with Disability Services for student-specific needs
▼

Action Create visual/tactile representations

  • Generate waveform visualizations, spectrograms, or graphic scores
  • These are transformative works — strong fair use case
  • Use color-coded notation for musical elements
  • Provide alternative text for all visual representations
  • Coordinate with Disability Services for individual accommodation plans

Transformative use under 17 U.S.C. § 107; ADA Title II reasonable accommodation

▼

Caution Standalone music has limited TEACH Act coverage

The TEACH Act limits use of sound recordings to "reasonable and limited portions." Full performances typically require licensing.

  • Check if your institution has streaming licenses (Naxos, Alexander Street, etc.)
  • Use licensed streaming platforms that provide their own accessibility features
  • For accessibility: provide a transcript of any spoken elements, descriptive program notes, and lyrics
  • If a D/deaf student needs to engage with the music, work with Disability Services for an individualized accommodation plan
  • Consider OER or Creative Commons-licensed alternatives

17 U.S.C. § 110(2) — limited to "reasonable and limited portions" for sound recordings

▼

✓ Document Everything

Regardless of the path you followed, always complete these documentation steps:

  • Fair use analysis: Record your four-factor analysis and date it
  • Accessibility verification: Confirm remediated content meets WCAG 2.1 AA
  • Access controls: Verify LMS restricts access to enrolled students
  • Citation: Properly cite the original work (see Citation Procedures tab)
  • Retention policy: Remove or re-evaluate remediated content when the course term ends
  • File storage: Keep fair use documentation, permission records, and remediation logs with course files

Citation Procedures for Remediated Copyrighted Media

When you caption, transcribe, or otherwise remediate copyrighted media for accessibility, you must cite the original work. Citations serve legal documentation purposes (demonstrating good faith) and academic integrity requirements. Follow APA 7th Edition format.

Required Within the LMS Course Page

  • Include a full citation adjacent to the media (on the same LMS page)
  • Add a copyright notice: "This material is copyrighted. It is provided here under [fair use / TEACH Act / license] for educational accessibility purposes only. Further distribution is prohibited."
  • Identify the remediation: "Captions/transcript added by [Institution] for accessibility compliance."

Citation Formats by Media Type

Video (APA 7th Edition)

Director, D. D. (Director). (Year). Title of video [Film/Documentary/Video]. Production Company. URL (if applicable) Accessibility note: Captions added by [University Name], [Date]. Original work used under fair use (17 U.S.C. § 107) for educational accessibility.

Podcast / Audio (APA 7th Edition)

Host, H. H. (Host). (Year, Month Day). Episode title (No. ##) [Audio podcast episode]. In Podcast Name. Publisher/Network. URL Accessibility note: Transcript created by [University Name], [Date], for accessibility under fair use (17 U.S.C. § 107).

Music (APA 7th Edition)

Artist, A. A. (Year). Song title [Song]. On Album title. Label. Accessibility note: Lyrics/descriptive analysis provided by [University Name], [Date], under fair use (17 U.S.C. § 107) for educational accessibility. Lyrics © [Songwriter/Publisher].

Within Caption/Transcript Files

  • Caption files (.srt, .vtt): Include a metadata note at the start of the file with the title, copyright holder, and remediation note
  • Transcript documents: Include a header with full citation and copyright/fair use notice
  • Audio description scripts: Note the original work's citation in the script header

Fair Use Documentation File

Maintain a separate fair use log for your department or course. For each remediated work, record:

  • Full bibliographic citation of the original
  • Date of fair use analysis
  • The four-factor analysis (brief rationale for each factor)
  • Whether you searched for an existing accessible version and the outcome
  • Who performed the remediation and what was done
  • Where the remediated file is stored and who has access
  • Planned review/removal date

ADA Title II Accessibility Accommodations for Public Universities

As a public university, your institution is subject to ADA Title II (28 CFR Part 35), Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, and the 2024 Title II Final Rule requiring WCAG 2.1 AA compliance. These obligations exist independently of copyright — ADA requirements are not waived by copyright restrictions.

Compliance deadline for entities serving populations of 50,000+: April 24, 2026

Proactive vs. Reactive Accommodations

View proactive obligations (Universal Design)

Proactive (required for all courses):

  • All pre-recorded video must have captions (WCAG 1.2.2)
  • All pre-recorded audio-only content must have a text transcript (WCAG 1.2.1)
  • Pre-recorded video must have audio description when visual content is not covered by the audio track (WCAG 1.2.5)
  • Live video and audio should have real-time captions when feasible
  • This applies to all content used in courses, including third-party and licensed content

2024 ADA Title II Final Rule; WCAG 2.1 AA; 28 CFR 35.200

Reactive accommodations (individual student requests)

When a student with a documented disability requests accommodation for specific media:

  • Process: Student registers with Disability Services → accommodation letter sent to instructor → instructor works with ID/Disability Services to remediate
  • Timeline: Provide accommodation in a timely manner; best practice is within the same timeframe as the assignment
  • Types of accommodations:
    • Captions or CART services for D/deaf or hard-of-hearing students
    • Audio description or tactile materials for blind/low-vision students
    • Extended time for media consumption
    • Alternative assignments when remediation is not feasible
    • Sign language interpretation for live content
  • Copyright note: Individual accommodations under ADA have the strongest legal protection. Courts have consistently held that accessibility obligations override copyright when content cannot be obtained in an accessible format

When Copyright and Accessibility Conflict

If a rights holder refuses permission to caption or transcribe, and no accessible version is available:

  • Step 1: Document your good-faith effort to obtain permission or an accessible version
  • Step 2: Consult your institution's Office of General Counsel (or equivalent) and Disability Services
  • Step 3: In most cases, your ADA Title II obligation will support proceeding with remediation under fair use. The accommodation is legally required.
  • Step 4: Apply maximum access restrictions (enrolled students only, no downloading, time-limited access)
  • Step 5: Consider replacing the content with an accessible alternative for future semesters

ADA Title II obligations are independent of copyright; see also Chafee Amendment (17 U.S.C. § 121) for print disabilities

The Chafee Amendment (17 U.S.C. § 121)

Signed into law in 1996 (P.L. 104-197), the Chafee Amendment allows authorized entities (including universities) to reproduce or distribute copyrighted works in specialized formats — including braille, audio, and digital text — for individuals with print disabilities, without permission from the copyright holder. While primarily applied to text, the principle extends to multimedia when serving students with qualifying disabilities. The 2018 Marrakesh Treaty Implementation Act expanded these protections.

Key Principles to Remember

1. ADA accessibility obligations exist independently of copyright and are not waived by copyright restrictions.

2. Always search for an existing accessible version before creating your own remediation.

3. Document every fair use decision in writing with a dated four-factor analysis.

4. Restrict access to remediated content to enrolled students; remove or re-evaluate at term end.

5. When in doubt, consult your institution's Office of General Counsel (or equivalent) and Disability Services.
